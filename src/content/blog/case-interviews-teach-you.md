Most people prepare for case interviews to get an offer. Fewer people notice
that the preparation itself teaches something durable: a repeatable way to
take an ambiguous problem, break it into testable pieces, and reason toward a
recommendation under uncertainty.

Structure is a tool, not a script. The frameworks — profitability trees,
market-sizing scaffolds, the classic "clarify, structure, analyze, recommend"
loop — are not meant to be recited. They are scaffolding you eventually
internalize and then discard once the underlying habit sticks: always know
what question you are actually answering before you start doing math.

What actually transfers: asking "so what?" after every finding, not just
producing analysis for its own sake. Being comfortable saying "I don't know
yet" and then designing a way to find out. Communicating a recommendation
before the supporting detail, not after.

None of this is unique to consulting case interviews. It is closer to how
any structured decision gets made well — which is probably why the format
has stuck around.

I'm still early in building this muscle, mostly through case practice and
coursework at SMU. This post will get more concrete as I have more reps to
draw on.
